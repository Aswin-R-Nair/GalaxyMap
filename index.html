About This Simulation

Overview

This is a 3D visualization of the Milky Way galaxy built with real stellar data from the ESA Gaia space observatory. Each point of light represents an actual star plotted at its calculated galactic position.

Controls

Orbit Mode (default) lets you rotate, pan, and zoom with the mouse. Explore Mode switches to first-person fly controls so you can navigate through the star field using WASD/RF keys. Use the speed slider to go from a few AU/s up to 10,000 pc/s.

Star Data

Stars are loaded from the Gaia catalog with real astrometric measurements: right ascension, declination, parallax, apparent magnitude (G-band), and color index (BP-RP). Equatorial coordinates are transformed into galactic coordinates so the galaxy's disk structure is visible.You will notice the stars are clustered around a certain point — that's where the Earth is located. Try going there and looking around, make sure to have a real image of the Milky Way from Earth pulled up to compare views.

Star Colors

Star colors are derived from the Gaia BP-RP color index, which measures the difference between blue and red photometer magnitudes. Hot blue-white O/B-type stars have low BP-RP values, while cool red M-type stars have high values. The colors are interpolated along a realistic blackbody radiation sequence.

Star Sizes & Brightness

Each star's on-screen size is based on its physical radius (scaled from 1 solar radius) with perspective projection - stars appear larger when closer. Brightness follows the inverse-square law using absolute magnitude, so intrinsically luminous stars appear brighter. The "Star Size Scale" slider multiplies the physical radius for visibility.Try flying around with 1x scale — you might not see many stars. Why is that?

Galaxy Rotation

The rotation speed slider animates differential galactic rotation. All stars share a base angular velocity, and the background Milky Way image rotates in sync. The simulation time can be paused with the checkbox.

The Sun

The green marker represents the Sun's position at approximately 8,200 parsecs from the galactic center. It orbits with the same angular velocity as the other stars.

Galactic Center

The golden ring at the origin marks the galactic center, representing the region around Sagittarius A*.

Image Overlay

The background image is an artist's impression of the Milky Way's spiral structure from above. The opacity slider lets you blend it with the real star data.
